\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}

This work applied the Pontryagin Maximum Principle to four minimum-fuel planar ascent programs of increasing complexity, all solved by single-shooting with \texttt{fsolve} over the costate initial conditions. Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarises the four programs at the common operating point $Q = 2$, $y_f = 0.04$; the main findings are discussed below.

The delivered payload goes $0.0241 \to 0.0231 \to 0.0458 \to 0.0680$ across the four programs, which shows the trade-offs at work: the vertical climb (Task~2) costs a few percent of payload compared with the bare burn (Task~1), the ballistic coast (Task~3) nearly doubles it ($+90\%$ over Task~1), and optimal staging (Task~4) almost triples it ($+182\%$). The main findings are summarized below.

\textbf{Bilinear-tangent law (all tasks).}\ The flat-Earth, drag-free dynamics produce a particularly tractable adjoint structure: four of the five costate components are determined analytically (two constants and one linear ramp), leaving only the mass costate $\lambda_m$ to be integrated numerically. The resulting bilinear-tangent steering law reduces the optimal control problem to a four-dimensional root-finding problem for the powered-arc programs (Tasks~1, 2 and~4) and a five-dimensional one for Task~3, where the mass costate is retained to evaluate the coast switching condition---making single-shooting computationally affordable in either case.

\textbf{Optimal thrust-to-weight ratio (Task 1).}\ The mass-flow rate $Q$ that maximises the final mass balances two competing losses: gravity losses (dominant at low $Q$, due to long burn times) and thrust-misalignment losses (dominant at high $Q$, due to steep climb angles). The optimal $Q^*$ shifts slightly with target altitude $y_f$, reflecting the longer burn time required for higher orbits.

\textbf{Continuation is essential.}\ Single shooting from a cold start fails across most of the $Q$ range. The continuation strategy---warm-starting each new $Q$ from the previous converged solution and sweeping forward and backward from a seed point---was the key ingredient that made the parameter sweeps in Tasks~1 and~4 possible.

\textbf{Coast arc saves propellant (Task 3).}\ Terminating the engine before reaching the target altitude and relying on ballistic dynamics is beneficial whenever the vehicle has sufficient residual vertical velocity to coast up to the injection point. The condition $S(t_c) = 0$ determines the optimal engine cutoff time without an additional outer optimization loop.

\textbf{Staging provides large payload gains (Task 4).}\ At the reference operating point ($Q = 2$, $y_f = 0.04$), the optimal two-stage configuration delivers a $+182\%$ payload gain over the single-stage baseline. Even the simplified constant-$Q$, drag-free model already captures most of this gain.

\textbf{Model limitations.}\ The simplifications used throughout (flat Earth, no drag, constant thrust, no vehicle attitude dynamics) leave out important physical effects. Atmospheric drag is large in the dense lower atmosphere---its density falls off with an $\approx 7$--$8$~km scale height in the standard exponential model, and capturing it needs an ascent benchmark that includes drag~\cite[Sec.~6.15]{betts2010}; Earth's curvature and rotation change the optimal coast and staging conditions; and real throttling constraints keep $Q$ away from its full theoretical range. Adding a drag model would shift $Q^*$ and $t_s^*$ in value and, more importantly, would change the whole structure of the indirect problem. Because drag depends on altitude (through $\rho$) and on speed (through $V$), the costates $\lambda_y, \lambda_{v_x}, \lambda_{v_y}$ would no longer be constant or linear: the closed-form adjoints and the bilinear-tangent law would be lost, and all five costates would need numerical integration with a stiffer shooting problem. The optimal thrust direction would still follow the primer vector ($\tan\varphi = \lambda_{v_y}/\lambda_{v_x}$, since drag does not depend on $\varphi$), and the PMP plus single-shooting machinery would still apply, but the analytic structure that makes the present drag-free problem so easy to solve is a direct consequence of neglecting aerodynamics.
