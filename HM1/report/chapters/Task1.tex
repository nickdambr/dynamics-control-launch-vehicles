\section{Task 1: Single Burn Arc}\label{sec:task1}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The simplest mission profile consists of a single continuous powered arc starting from rest at the origin, $(x, y, v_x, v_y) = (0, 0, 0, 0)$ with $m(0) = 1$, and ending at the orbital injection state satisfying Eq.~\eqref{eq:terminal}. The mass-flow rate $Q$ is the primary free parameter swept over the range $Q \in [1.8, 7]$; values below $Q_\mathrm{min} = 1/c \approx 1.67$ would yield insufficient thrust at liftoff ($\left.T/W\right|_{t=0} = cQ < 1$).

\subsection{Boundary Value Problem Formulation}

The four shooting unknowns $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:unknowns}) determine the costate initial conditions and the free flight time. The shooting residual, coded in the function \texttt{shooting1} of \texttt{main\_task1.m} (Listing~\ref{lst:shooting}), is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{r}_1(\boldsymbol{\zeta}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        y(t_f) - y_f \\[2pt]
        v_x(t_f) - 1 \\[2pt]
        v_y(t_f) \\[2pt]
        \mathcal{H}(0)
    \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{0}.
    \label{eq:residual1}
\end{equation}
The first three components enforce the terminal state constraints \eqref{eq:terminal}; the fourth is the free-time condition $\mathcal{H} = 0$, imposed at $t_0$ through the algebraic form \eqref{eq:H0}. The mass-costate transversality $\lambda_m(t_f) = 1$ and the terminal value of $\mathcal{H}$ are no longer needed as residuals, having been folded into the $\lambda_{m,0} = 1$ normalization and the autonomy of the system.

\subsection{Velocity-Loss Decomposition}

The achieved injection speed falls short of the ideal Tsiolkovsky delta-V,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta V_\mathrm{ideal} = c\ln\!\frac{m_0}{m_f} = c\ln\!\frac{1}{m_f}
    = \int_0^{t_f}\!\frac{T}{m}\,\mathrm{d}t,
    \label{eq:dvideal}
\end{equation}
where the last equality follows from the mass dynamics $\dot{m} = -T/c$. The shortfall is the total velocity loss $W_\mathrm{tot} = \Delta V_\mathrm{ideal} - v_f$, with $v_f = 1$. To resolve it into physical contributions, project the equations of motion onto the velocity unit vector: with flight-path angle $\psi = \arctan(v_y/v_x)$ and thrust angle $\varphi$, the speed evolves as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert}{\mathrm{d}t}
    = \frac{T}{m}\cos(\varphi - \psi) - \sin\psi,
    \label{eq:speed_rate}
\end{equation}
the first term being the thrust projected along the velocity and the second gravity (non-dimensional $g = 1$) projected along it. Integrating from rest ($\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert_0 = 0$) to injection ($\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert_{t_f} = v_f$) and subtracting the result from $\Delta V_\mathrm{ideal}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:dvideal} yields the exact identity
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{\Delta V_\mathrm{ideal} - v_f}_{W_\mathrm{tot}}
    = \underbrace{\int_0^{t_f}\!\frac{T}{m}\bigl[1 - \cos(\varphi - \psi)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t}_{W_d}
    + \underbrace{\int_0^{t_f}\!\sin\psi\,\mathrm{d}t}_{W_g},
    \label{eq:loss_split}
\end{equation}
which splits the loss into a thrust-misalignment term and a gravity term,
\begin{align}
    W_d &= \int_0^{t_f} \frac{T}{m}\bigl[1 - \cos(\varphi - \psi)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t
         && \text{(thrust misalignment loss)},
    \label{eq:Wd}\\
    W_g &= \int_0^{t_f} \sin\psi\,\mathrm{d}t
         && \text{(gravity loss)}.
    \label{eq:Wg}
\end{align}
The misalignment loss $W_d$ collects the fraction of thrust not aligned with the velocity vector (it vanishes only when $\varphi \equiv \psi$); the gravity loss $W_g$ is the component of gravity opposing the motion, integrated along the trajectory.

\subsection{Results}\label{sec:task1_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:task1a} shows the final mass as a function of $Q$ for the three target altitudes, obtained by solving the BVP at each $Q$ with the continuation sweep of \texttt{main\_task1.m} (Listing~\ref{lst:continuation}). Each curve has a clear interior maximum $Q^*$: at low $Q$ the long burn time builds up large gravity losses, while at high $Q$ the vehicle climbs steeply and the thrust-misalignment loss takes over.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{task1_task_1a_final_mass_vs_q}
    \caption{Task 1a: final mass $m_f$ as a function of the mass-flow rate $Q$ for three target altitudes $y_f$.}
    \label{fig:task1a}
\end{figure}

The interior maxima are listed in Table~\ref{tab:qstar}: the optimal rate $Q^\ast$ decreases from $2.52$ to $2.19$ as the target altitude rises from $y_f = 0.04$ to $0.06$, and the delivered payload becomes negative at $y_f = 0.06$. A single stage cannot reach that orbit with positive payload, which is what motivates the staging in Task~4. The nominal $Q = 2$ of the assignment sits a bit below $Q^\ast$, but the $m_f(Q)$ curve is flat near its peak, so the final mass at $Q = 2$ is still within a few percent of the optimum.
