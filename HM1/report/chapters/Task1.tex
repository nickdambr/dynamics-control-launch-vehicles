\section{Task 1: Single Burn Arc}\label{sec:task1}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

The simplest mission profile consists of a single continuous powered arc starting from rest at the origin, $(x, y, v_x, v_y) = (0, 0, 0, 0)$ with $m(0) = 1$, and ending at the orbital injection state satisfying Eq.~\eqref{eq:terminal}. The mass-flow rate $Q$ is the primary free parameter swept over the range $Q \in [1.8, 7]$; values below $Q_\mathrm{min} = 1/c \approx 1.67$ would yield insufficient thrust at liftoff ($\left.T/W\right|_{t=0} = cQ < 1$).

\subsection{Boundary Value Problem Formulation}

The five shooting unknowns $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:unknowns}) determine both the costate initial conditions and the free flight time. The shooting residual is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{r}_1(\boldsymbol{\zeta}) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        y(t_f) - y_f \\[2pt]
        v_x(t_f) - 1 \\[2pt]
        v_y(t_f) \\[2pt]
        \lambda_m(t_f) - 1 \\[2pt]
        \mathcal{H}(t_f)
    \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{0},
    \label{eq:residual1}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}(t_f)$ is computed via Eq.~\eqref{eq:ham_opt}. The first three components enforce the terminal state constraints \eqref{eq:terminal}; the fourth is the transversality condition \eqref{eq:trans_m} on the mass costate; the fifth is the free-time condition \eqref{eq:trans_tf}.

\subsection{Velocity-Loss Decomposition}

The total velocity loss $W_\mathrm{tot} = \Delta V_\mathrm{ideal} - v_f$ compares the ideal Tsiolkovsky delta-V,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta V_\mathrm{ideal} = c\ln\!\frac{m_0}{m_f} = c\ln\!\frac{1}{m_f},
\end{equation}
against the achieved injection speed $v_f = 1$. It is decomposed as
\begin{align}
    W_d &= \int_0^{t_f} \frac{T}{m}\bigl[1 - \cos(\varphi - \psi)\bigr]\,\mathrm{d}t
         && \text{(thrust misalignment loss)},
    \label{eq:Wd}\\
    W_g &= \int_0^{t_f} \sin\psi\,\mathrm{d}t
         && \text{(gravity loss)},
    \label{eq:Wg}
\end{align}
where $\psi = \arctan(v_y/v_x)$ is the flight-path angle. Misalignment loss $W_d$ arises from the fraction of thrust not aligned with the velocity vector; gravity loss $W_g$ is the work done against gravity projected along the trajectory.

\subsection{Results}\label{sec:task1_results}

Figure~\ref{fig:task1a} shows the final mass as a function of $Q$ for the three target altitudes. Each curve exhibits a clear interior maximum $Q^*$: at low $Q$, the long burn time accumulates large gravity losses; at high $Q$, the vehicle climbs steeply and thrust-misalignment loss dominates.

Figure~\ref{fig:task1b} confirms this trade-off by decomposing the velocity loss at $y_f = 0.04$. The gravity loss $W_g$ is large at low $Q$ and decreases monotonically with $Q$, while the misalignment loss $W_d$ has a broad minimum near $Q^*$.

Figures~\ref{fig:task1c_traj} and~\ref{fig:task1c_angles} show the optimal trajectory and control history at $Q = Q^*$ for $y_f = 0.04$. The thrust angle $\varphi$ decreases smoothly from near-vertical at launch to near-horizontal at injection, following the bilinear-tangent law \eqref{eq:bilinear}. The velocity angle $\psi$ tracks $\varphi$ closely once the initial transient has died out.
