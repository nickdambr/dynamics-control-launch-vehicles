\section{Task 2 --- Full Launch Vehicle Model}\label{sec:task2}

Task~2 reintroduces the full dynamics of Eq.~\eqref{eq:plant}: the lightly damped
first bending mode, the second-order TVC actuator of Eq.~\eqref{eq:tvc} with its
$20$~ms delay, and the INS measurement model of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ins}. The design
proceeds incrementally, as suggested in the assignment guidelines, with the PD
gains of Task~1 kept fixed throughout (the guideline note anticipates that
retuning is usually unnecessary, and this is confirmed below).

\subsection{Step B --- uncompensated full model}

Adding the TVC, delay and bending mode to the Task~1 controller leaves the
margins at low frequency essentially intact, but the bending resonance appears
in the loop at a magnitude of $|L(\omega_{BM})| = +39$~dB. With a damping of only
$\zeta_{BM} = 0.005$ this peak encircles the critical point and the closed loop
becomes \textbf{unstable} (a closed-loop pole at $\mathrm{Re}(s)=+5.6$). The red
curve in Figure~\ref{fig:t2nichols} shows the bending loop crossing into the
unstable region.

\subsection{Step C --- bending filter trade study}

The guideline filter of Eq.~4 admits two readings: as printed (negative
numerator damping, a non-minimum-phase \emph{lead-lag} that phase-stabilises the
resonance by rotating it on the Nichols chart) or in its minimum-phase variant
(a band-reject \emph{notch} that gain-stabilises the resonance by driving it
below $0$~dB). Rather than committing a priori, four candidates are compared
with the same fixed PD gains; Table~\ref{tab:filters} collects the resulting
margins (rigid-body and minimum gain margin, phase margin, delay margin on top
of the $20$~ms already modelled, and the residual loop gain at the bending
frequency).

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{C-LL --- Eq.~4 lead-lag alone.} The filter is swept over the
ranges suggested in the assignment ($\zeta_{x,N} \in [0.1,0.3]$,
$\zeta_{x,D} \in [0.4,0.6]$, $\omega_x = \omega_{BM} \pm 4$~rad/s,
$75$ combinations): \emph{none} stabilises the loop. The least-unstable
combination ($\omega_x = 18.9$, $\zeta_{x,N}=0.10$, $\zeta_{x,D}=0.60$) still
leaves a closed-loop pole at $\mathrm{Re}(s) = +0.16$ --- the green curve in
Figure~\ref{fig:t2nichols} shows its bending lobe still pinned to the critical
region. Phase rotation alone cannot rescue a $+39$~dB resonance here.
\item \textbf{C-N --- deep minimum-phase notch.} Gain stabilisation needs more
than $39$~dB of attenuation at $\omega_{BM}$, which forces the numerator
damping far below the suggested range: $\zeta_{x,N}=0.002$, $\zeta_{x,D}=0.7$
give a $-51$~dB null and drive $|L(\omega_{BM})|$ to $-12$~dB. The depth follows
the closed-form attenuation of a second-order notch,
$K_\mathrm{notch} = 20\log_{10}(\zeta_{x,N}/\zeta_{x,D})$, so that
$20\log_{10}(0.002/0.7) \approx -51$~dB at
$\omega_{BM}$~\cite[Eq.~(3.18)]{trotta2021}; pulling a $+39$~dB peak below
$0$~dB requires $\zeta_{x,N}/\zeta_{x,D} \le 10^{-39/20} \approx 0.011$, i.e.\
$\zeta_{x,N} \lesssim 0.008$ with $\zeta_{x,D} = 0.7$, which is precisely why the
sizing must leave the assignment's suggested $\zeta_{x,N}\in[0.1,0.3]$ range. The null is
deliberately narrow because the bending frequency sits only a decade above the
rigid crossover ($\omega_{BM}/\omega_c \approx 8$): a wide band-reject section
would leak phase lag into the conditionally stable low-frequency region.
\item \textbf{C-T --- notch triplet.} The notch-triplet recipe against
bending-frequency uncertainty places three cascaded notches at
$\{0.9,\,1.0,\,1.1\}\,\omega_{BM}$~\cite[Eq.~(3.19), Fig.~3.11]{trotta2021}.
Sized to the same depth, however, the three wide ($\zeta_{x,D}=0.7$) sections
pile up about $30^\circ$ of phase lag at the rigid crossover and the
conditionally stable loop goes \emph{unstable at nominal} ($|PM|$ collapses to
$11.6^\circ$). This is consistent with the course philosophy: the triplet
belongs to the higher, well-separated modes, while the \emph{first} mode --- too
close to the control band --- must be treated either with an exact notch or by
phase stabilisation.
\item \textbf{C-NLL --- notch $+$ lead-lag.} Reading the guideline literally
(``the Notch filter and possibly other filters''), the deep notch is cascaded
with the Eq.~4 lead-lag, the partner swept over the same ranges: $31/75$
combinations now stabilise the loop. The best one ($\omega_x = 22.9$,
$\zeta_{x,N}=0.10$, $\zeta_{x,D}=0.40$, selected for maximum delay margin) is
stable but with visibly thinner nominal margins ($|PM| = 9.2^\circ$,
$\min|GM| = 0.7$~dB). Its merit appears in the robustness analysis below.
\end{itemize}

The \textbf{deep notch is retained}: it gain-stabilises the resonance while
preserving the rigid-body margins ($|GM| = 6.6$~dB, $|PM| = 26.5^\circ$ ---
a few degrees below the $30^\circ$ of the ideal-actuator loop of Task~1, the
difference being the phase lag of the second-order TVC and of the $20$~ms
transport delay), leaves a $58.8$~ms delay margin, and requires no retuning of
the PD gains, consistent with the guideline note. The bending mode itself is
left at its lightly damped but stable open-loop location. One caveat is
recorded: the minimum gain margin of the stabilised loop, governed by the
bending shoulder, is $1.8$~dB --- adequate for this exercise but well below the
$\ge 12$~dB of gain margin (and $45^\circ$ of phase margin) customarily required
of gain-stabilised \emph{bending} modes, as distinct from the $6$~dB$/30^\circ$
rigid-body figure~\cite[p.~279]{barrows2020}, the heightened bending requirement
reflecting the larger modelling uncertainty and latency sensitivity at high
frequency. The deeper margin would be bought with a stiffer notch or a retuned
loop gain.

\subsection{Sensitivity to the bending-frequency knowledge}

The deep notch buys its performance with a $-51$~dB null only $0.08$~rad/s
wide, i.e.\ with the assumption that $\omega_{BM}$ is known exactly --- true in
this exercise, where the LPV dataset provides it, but never true in practice,
which is precisely why the course notes prescribe the $\pm 10\%$ triplet.
Table~\ref{tab:wbm} makes the trade explicit: each filter is held fixed while
the true plant bending frequency is perturbed. The notch tolerates barely
$-5\%$; the notch$+$lead-lag combination, by phase-stabilising whatever leaks
past the (detuned) notch, recovers nearly the full $\pm 10\%$ band at the price
of the thin nominal margins seen above. In a flight design, where
$\omega_{BM}$ drifts with propellant depletion, the robust variant (or a
retuned phase-stabilised loop) would be mandatory; for the present max-$\bar q$
snapshot the exact notch is retained.

\subsection{Validation and comparison with the rigid model}

The gust response of the stabilised full model is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:t2gust}; all four assignment time histories remain well damped,
with peaks $\theta = 0.132^\circ$, $z = 2.37$~m, $\delta = 0.39^\circ$ and a
load indicator $\bar q\,\alpha = 24.9$~kPa\,deg, marginally below the rigid
case. Because the notch is localised at the bending frequency and the TVC
bandwidth ($70$~rad/s) is well above the control crossover, the full-model
response is virtually indistinguishable from the rigid one
(Figure~\ref{fig:t2cmp}): the added dynamics affect stability robustness (the
bending and delay margins) far more than nominal performance.

\subsection{A note on the tail-wags-dog zero}

A second flexible effect bounds what any filter can achieve. The gimballed
nozzle has its own inertia, so its angular acceleration reacts back on the
airframe: at the \emph{tail-wags-dog} (TWD) frequency
\begin{equation}
\omega_{TWD}^2 = -\frac{F_R X_G}{I_{EG} + X_G m_E l_E},
\label{eq:twd}
\end{equation}
nozzle deflection produces zero net vehicle response, a transmission zero that
caps the achievable closed-loop bandwidth~\cite[Eq.~(6.1.11)]{barrows2020}. The
$-\phi_{1,TVC}T_c$ entry in the bending-forcing column of Eq.~\eqref{eq:plant} is
the flexible-mode analogue of this nozzle-reaction term. The present model
treats the actuator as a stiff second-order block (Eq.~\eqref{eq:tvc}) and so
neglects the nozzle back-reaction --- the \emph{dog-wags-tail} feedback, which
would add the gimbal angle as an extra state; a higher-fidelity design would
retain it, one of the extensions noted in the conclusions.
