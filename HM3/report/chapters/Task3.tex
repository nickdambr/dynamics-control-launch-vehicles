\section{Task 3 --- Robustness to Parametric Uncertainty}\label{sec:task3}

The aerodynamic moment $\mu_\alpha = A_6$ and the control effectiveness
$\mu_c = K_1$ are treated as uncertain. The controller designed in Task~2
(PD gains plus bending notch) is held \textbf{fixed} and its robustness is
assessed over the \emph{four corner cases} of the uncertainty box, i.e.\ the
vertices V1--V4 obtained by independently varying $\mu_\alpha$ and $\mu_c$ by
$\pm 30\%$. Since each vertex perturbs both parameters at once, the four
one-at-a-time variations S1--S4 ($\pm 30\%$ on a single parameter) are also
evaluated: they sit on the edge midpoints of the box rather than on its
corners, but they cleanly attribute the observed effects to each parameter.

\subsection{Frequency- and time-domain analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:corners} collects, for every case, the rigid-body
(low-frequency) gain margin, the smallest gain margin over the whole loop
(governed by the bending shoulder), the phase margin, the delay margin, and
the peak pitch and lateral-drift excursions to the same severe gust.
\emph{All vertices remain stable}: the fixed Task-2 controller tolerates the
entire $\pm 30\%$ uncertainty box without re-design.

\subsection{Discussion}

The sensitivities S1--S4 attribute the effects: $\mu_\alpha$ moves the unstable
airframe pole ($\pm 30\%$ shifts the rigid margin by roughly $\mp 2$--$3$~dB
and scales the gust excursions), while $\mu_c$ scales the whole loop gain
($+30\%$ improves the rigid margin but pushes the gain-stabilised bending
shoulder $2.3$~dB closer to $0$~dB --- the leading-order $20\log_{10}1.3 = 2.28$~dB
loop-gain shift, only approximate because $K_1$ also scales the bending-forcing
column of Eq.~\eqref{eq:plant} and not merely the loop gain --- and halves the
delay margin). The vertices
then combine these mechanisms:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{V3} ($\mu_\alpha\!\uparrow$, $\mu_c\!\downarrow$) is the
\emph{worst case for the rigid loop and for performance}, as the two effects
compound: the airframe is more unstable \emph{and} the authority to fight it is
reduced. The rigid gain margin collapses to $1.5$~dB with $|PM| = 9.1^\circ$,
and the gust excursions grow to $0.35^\circ$ in pitch and $4.2$~m of drift ---
roughly $60\%$ beyond the worst one-at-a-time case (S3), which is why testing
the actual vertices, and not only the single-parameter variations, is
essential.
\item \textbf{V2 and V4} ($\mu_c\!\uparrow$) are the \emph{worst cases for the
bending channel}: the loop-gain increase squeezes the minimum gain margin to
$0.9$~dB and the delay margin to ${\sim}20$~ms. The binding constraint in
these corners is the flexible side of the loop, not the rigid one.
\item \textbf{V1} ($\mu_\alpha\!\downarrow$, $\mu_c\!\downarrow$) is benign on
both counts: the two reductions nearly cancel in the rigid loop and relax the
bending shoulder.
\end{itemize}

In summary, performance degradation is driven by $\mu_\alpha$ and mitigated by
$\mu_c$, stability of the bending channel is eroded by $\mu_c$, and the
worst-case combination V3 leaves single-digit margins. Because the margins are
non-monotonic in $\mu_c$ (it helps the rigid loop while hurting the bending
shoulder), the global worst case over the $\pm 30\%$ box is not guaranteed to
sit on a vertex: the four corners and the one-at-a-time sensitivities are a
screening, and a denser grid or Monte-Carlo sweep would be needed to certify an
interior worst case. The fixed classical design survives the whole box --- but a
requirement of, say, $\ge 3$~dB and $\ge 10^\circ$ in every corner would already
call for either gain scheduling on $\bar q$ or a robust-synthesis retune, the
standard routes beyond a single fixed classical design~\cite{trotta2021}, which
is the practical takeaway of this exercise.
