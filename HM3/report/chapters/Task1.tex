\subsection{Rigid model and loop}

Neglecting the bending mode, the plant reduces to the four states
$[\,z,\dot z,\theta,\dot\theta\,]$ (\texttt{build\_plant\_rigid.m}, Listing~\ref{lst:hm3_plant}) and the actuator is taken as ideal:
$W_{TVC}(s)=1$ and no transport delay. The open-loop transfer broken at the TVC
deflection, in the $1+L$ convention, is obtained by closing the static
PD law of Eq.~\eqref{eq:control} around the plant (\texttt{assemble\_loop}, Listing~\ref{lst:hm3_loop}):
\begin{equation}
L(s) = K_{P,\theta}\,G_{\delta\to\theta}(s)
     + K_{D,\theta}\,G_{\delta\to\dot\theta}(s)
     + K_{P,z}\,G_{\delta\to z}(s)
     + K_{D,z}\,G_{\delta\to\dot z}(s),
\end{equation}
where $G_{\delta\to\bullet}$ are the plant channels from $\delta$ to each
measurement. The dominant term is the pitch loop
$L_\theta(s) = (K_{P,\theta}+K_{D,\theta}s)\,K_1/(s^2-A_6)$, whose closed-loop
characteristic polynomial $s^2 + K_1 K_{D,\theta}\,s + (K_1 K_{P,\theta}-A_6)$
is stable only for $K_{P,\theta} > A_6/K_1 = 0.74$ and $K_{D,\theta} > 0$, so the
proportional gain has to overcome the aerodynamic instability. The rigid loop is
sketched in Figure~\ref{fig:loop_rigid}.

The gain and phase margins quoted below are read on the \emph{loop transfer}
$L(s)$, obtained by \textbf{opening this loop at the TVC deflection}~$\delta$
(Figure~\ref{fig:loopbreak}): $\delta$ is the single actuator signal, so cutting
the wire there, injecting a test signal $\delta_{in}$ into the plant and reading
the returning $\delta_{out}$ at the actuator output gives a scalar
$L(s)=\delta_{out}/\delta_{in}$. This is why the Nichols charts are labelled
``\,from $\delta$ to $\delta$\,'': input and output sit at the same break point.
Closed-loop poles are the roots of $1+L=0$.

\subsection{Tuning}

The pitch gains follow the classical launcher design of the course
notes~\cite[Lecture~16]{dclvnotes}. Starting from the canonical closed form on
the decoupled rotational dynamics $\ddot\theta = A_6\theta + K_1\delta$,
\begin{equation}
K_{P,\theta}^{(0)} = \frac{2A_6}{K_1}, \qquad
K_{D,\theta}^{(0)} = \frac{\sqrt{A_6}}{K_1},
\label{eq:canon}
\end{equation}
which place the pitch pair at $\omega_c=\sqrt{A_6}$, $\zeta=0.5$ and give a
$6$~dB gain margin and $30^\circ$ phase margin \emph{on that decoupled loop}.
On the full four-state loop the lateral-drift feedback erodes the aerodynamic
gain margin (the $6$~dB drops to about $4$~dB), so a two-parameter
\texttt{fminsearch} retunes $(K_{P,\theta},K_{D,\theta})$ on the \emph{full}
loop until the frequency-classified aerodynamic gain margin and rigid-body phase
margin meet the assignment targets. The lateral gains stay fixed at
$K_{P,z}=K_{D,z}=-10^{-3}$ (load relief, not a stability margin). The result is
\begin{equation}
K_{P,\theta} = 1.78,\quad K_{D,\theta} = 0.44,\quad
K_{P,z}=K_{D,z}=-10^{-3},
\end{equation}
with, read on the full loop, an aerodynamic gain margin $|GM_a| = 6.0$~dB at
$0.59$~rad/s, a rigid-body phase margin $PM = 30^\circ$ at $2.45$~rad/s, and a
delay margin of $213$~ms.
In the pole-placement language of the course notes~\cite[Lecture~15]{dclvnotes}
these gains place the pitch pair at $\omega_c = \sqrt{K_1 K_{P,\theta}-A_6} =
2.18$~rad/s --- inside the usual $1$--$4$~rad/s control band --- with damping
$\zeta = K_1 K_{D,\theta}/(2\omega_c) = 0.46$, in the standard $0.4$--$0.7$
range. These relations invert the closed-loop characteristic polynomial, matching
$s^2 + K_1 K_{D,\theta}\,s + (K_1 K_{P,\theta} - A_6)$ to $s^2 + 2\zeta\omega_c s + \omega_c^2$:
\begin{equation}
K_{P,\theta} = \frac{\omega_c^2 + A_6}{K_1}, \qquad
K_{D,\theta} = \frac{2\zeta\omega_c}{K_1}.
\label{eq:poleplace}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Reading the margins.} The margins are read on the \emph{full} open
loop and classified by frequency band, not taken from \texttt{margin}'s default
crossover~\cite{barrowsorr}. Because the lateral-drift position feedback carries
a free integrator ($z/\delta\to\infty$ at DC), the Nichols curve comes from the
top (Figure~\ref{fig:t1nichols}): the aerodynamic gain margin is the
low-frequency gain-reduction margin, read where the curve crosses the critical
point --- $(-180^\circ, 0~\mathrm{dB})$, from $1+L=0$ --- at $|GM_a|=6$~dB, and
the rigid-body phase margin is read at
the $2.45$~rad/s gain crossover. With the ideal Task-1 actuator these are the
only rigid-body margins; the high-frequency rigid-body gain margin (a
gain-increase margin set by the actuator lag) and the bending Flex margins appear
once the TVC servo, transport delay and notch are added in Task~2. Crossings
below $\sim0.5$~rad/s are lateral-drift artifacts, not rigid-body margins.

In the $s$-plane the same design reads as a pole placement
(Figure~\ref{fig:t1pz}): the PD gains pull the unstable $+1.84$~rad/s aerodynamic
pole into the left half-plane, leaving a well-damped fast pitch pair
($-0.95\pm1.90i$, $\zeta\approx0.45$) and a slow, lightly damped drift pair
($-0.06\pm0.23i$) close to the imaginary axis --- the stability margin the
Nichols chart quantifies.

\subsection{Wind-gust response}

The closed loop is excited by a deterministic ``1-cosine'' wind gust whose
amplitude ($V_g = 6.4$~m/s) is taken from the reference dry-wind dispersion
(\texttt{drywind.mat}, severe regime) at the current altitude, converted to a
wind angle of attack $\alpha_w = v_w/V$ with a peak of $0.39^\circ$. The four
key time histories requested by the assignment are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:t1gust}: the pitch attitude peaks at $0.26^\circ$, the TVC
deflection at $0.53^\circ$, and the lateral drift peaks at $2.3$~m before the
slow, lightly damped drift mode ($\zeta \approx 0.23$, $\omega_n \approx 0.24$~rad/s)
returns it to trim. All four states decay back to zero within about $80$~s ---
confirming the loop is asymptotically stable --- and the actuator effort stays
small.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{task1_gust_response.png}
\caption{Task 1 --- response to a severe wind gust: pitch attitude $\theta$,
lateral drift $z$, drift rate $\dot z$ and TVC deflection $\delta$.}
\label{fig:t1gust}
\end{figure}

Since max-$\bar q$ is the load-critical point of the trajectory, the total
angle of attack $\alpha = \theta + \dot z/V - \alpha_w$ and the structural-load
indicator $\bar q\,\alpha$ are also monitored (Figure~\ref{fig:t1alpha}). The
minus sign is the model's own: $\alpha$ is the incidence seen by the
\emph{air-relative} velocity, and it is the quantity that multiplies $A_6$ and
$a_1 V$ in Eq.~(1), whose wind column is
$\mathbf{B}_w = [0,\,-a_1V,\,0,\,-A_6,\,0,\,0]^\top$.
To hold attitude against the gust the loop pitches the vehicle into the relative
wind ($\theta \to -0.26^\circ$ at the gust peak), and that contribution
\emph{adds} to the wind's own rather than cancelling it: the peak total
incidence, $0.577^\circ$, therefore \emph{exceeds} the $0.39^\circ$ of the gust
alone. With $\bar q = 81$~kPa the peak load indicator is
$\bar q\,\alpha = 47$~kPa\,deg. A pure attitude-hold law is thus mildly
\emph{load aggravating} --- which is precisely why flight designs add an explicit
load-relief term. The incidence nevertheless stays well below the few-degree
limit typical of a slender launcher: taking a representative allowable
$\alpha_{\lim} = 4^\circ$ (the assignment specifies no vehicle allowable), the
load limit is $\bar q\,\alpha_{\lim} \simeq 325$~kPa\,deg --- drawn as the
dashed pair in Figure~\ref{fig:t1alpha} --- and the gust peak sits a factor
$6.9$ below it, so at this gust level the design is not load critical.

This drift-versus-load tension is the standard trade in launch-vehicle wind
control~\cite[Sec.~3.1]{greensite_cr826}. Applying the final-value theorem to a
\emph{sustained} step gust, with the attitude loop holding $\theta_{ss} = 0$, the
aerodynamic moment can only vanish if the incidence does: $\alpha_{ss} = 0$, which
forces a steady drift rate $\dot z_{ss} = +V\,\alpha_w$. The vehicle is simply blown
downwind until it flies \emph{with} the air --- it nulls the relative incidence but
not the dispersion. The 1-cosine gust of Figure~\ref{fig:t1gust} is instead a
transient pulse, so once it has passed the stable loop returns the drift to zero,
but only through the slow, lightly damped mode, which is why the settling takes
$\sim\!80$~s.

The weak negative drift gains are, first of all, a \emph{stability} requirement
rather than a load-relief device. Lateral position has no restoring force: the
rigid airframe carries a free integrator in $z$, and with $K_{P,z}=K_{D,z}=0$ the
closed loop keeps a pole exactly at the origin --- poles
$\{0,\ -0.076,\ -0.98 \pm 1.93\mathrm{i}\}$, marginally stable, with $z$ never
returning to zero. The $-10^{-3}$ gains move that pair to
$-0.056 \pm 0.233\mathrm{i}$, cutting the peak drift from $9.5$~m to $2.3$~m while
changing the peak incidence by barely $1\%$ ($0.584^\circ \to 0.577^\circ$). They
buy drift control, not load relief. The \textbf{load-minimum} condition of the
classical literature ($K_{P,\theta} = 0$, so that $\alpha_{ss} = 0$) is moreover
inadmissible on this airframe: $K_{P,\theta}$ is precisely what stabilises the
$+\sqrt{A_6}$ aerodynamic root, and setting it to zero makes the loop diverge.
Genuine load relief on an unstable launcher must therefore come from an added
feedback, not from detuning the attitude channel: the classical options are a
direct incidence term $K_\alpha\alpha$ (needs an air-data measurement or
estimate), a lateral-acceleration term $K_a\ddot z$, or larger translational
gains on $(z,\dot z)$ --- the latter being the structure used here, but sized
for drift control rather than load relief.
